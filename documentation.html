User Manual

Introduction

The Wilde Trials International Archive is a textbase and text-sharing detection program for analyzing international news reports about the three trials and imprisonment of Oscar Wilde. Wilde’s three trials were an international media event in the spring of 1895 and were front­page news the world over for nearly a two­month period, triggering debates about sexuality, censorship, and press standards. The textbase contains news reports published in 1895 which have been gathered from newspapers around the world in a number of languages.

Pages

Home Page

The Home Page offers access to the other pages on the site through in-text links (indicated in red) and through header and footer menus.

To return to the home page at any time, select Wilde Trials International News Archive at the top left-hand corner.

About

Select “About” from the header menu to choose a page from the drop-down menu.

  • The Overview page provides background on Oscar Wilde’s three trials and the website’s features.
  • The Curatorial Statement provides information on the data sources and data analysis methods used.
  • The News Sources page provides a full list of all sources for the news reports, which can be browsed by source.
  • The Image Gallery contains selected images of the trials from the news reports.
  • The Contributors page lists all contributors to the Wilde Trials International News Archive.

Browsing the Reports

Select “Reports” from the header menu to choose a page from the drop-down menu.

  • The All Reports page provides a full list of all news reports collected related to the three trials and imprisonment of Oscar Wilde. Each row in the list provides basic metadata about an individual report:

    • Headline: the headline of the individual report, which provides a link to the report.
    • Date: the publication date of the report.
    • Newspaper: the name of the newspaper in which the report was published.
    • Region: the country in which the newspaper was published.
    • City: the city in which the newspaper was published.
    • Language: the language in which the report was first published.
    • Document Matches: the number of other full reports that match, based on the measure of Levenshtein distance.
    • Paragraph Matches: the number of other paragraphs in other reports that match, based on the measure of Levenshtein distance.
    • Words: the word count of the report, excluding the headline.

  • The By City page allows for browsing news reports by city.
  • The By Date page allows for browsing news reports by date.
  • The By Language page allows for browsing news reports by language.
  • The By Newspaper page allows for browsing news reports by newspaper.
  • The By Region page allows for browsing news reports by region.

Individual Reports

Each report provides two different kinds of matching tools. The main section shows in-line matches, which are matches to other news reports that are found at the paragraph level.

The second comparison section, on the right-hand side, shows document matches, which are matches to other news reports that are found at the full document level.

Each report also includes basic bibliographic metadata about the newspaper from which it was derived, as well as date of publication, word count, and source information. If sourced from open-access databases, a link to the facsimile is provided. In the case of certain databases, however, it is not possible to provide a direct link to the report.

Metadata for each individual report includes:

  • Newspaper: the name of the newspaper in which the report was published
  • Date published: the publication date of the report
  • Region: the country and city in which the newspaper was published
  • Original language: the language in which the report was first published
  • Sources: the database and institution where the newspaper is located, including links
  • Facsimile: link to the digitized copy of the report
  • Word count: the number of words in the report

In-line matches

The in-line match section will be shown first in the language in which it was originally published. For non-English languages, a translation in English can be found by selecting the “English” tab. Matches may be found in either the original or translated language.

Each paragraph from the individual report that matches a paragraph in another report will appear with a match button next to it. The match button will indicate how many matches of that paragraph have been found.

Selecting the match button will display the matches of each paragraph.

Selecting Compare Paragraphs will open a new page that compares the two reports at the paragraph level. The page displays the original paragraph(s) on the left, and the matching paragraph(s) in the middle. The column on the right highlights any differences between the two matching paragraphs: pink shows differences in the first report and purple in the second report.

Selecting Compare Documents will open a new page that compares the two reports at the document level. The page displays the original document on the left, and the matching document in the middle. The column on the right highlights any differences between the two matching documents: pink shows differences in the first report and purple in the second report.

Document Matches:

To the right of the in-line comparison tool is the document comparison tool. The tool lists the names of each report that matches the original report at the document level, and the percentage of text-matching between documents. It also includes a link to a document comparison tool. Selecting Compare Documents will open a new page that compares the two reports at the document level.

Levenshtein matches indicate reports that match based onthe number of different characters between two pieces of text.

Selecting the news report title will take you to the individual record for that report.

Selecting the Compare tool will open a new page that compares the two reports at the document level. The page displays the original document on the left, and the matching document in the middle. The column on the right highlights any differences between the two matching documents: pink shows differences in the first report and purple in the second report.

Searching the Reports

Use the "Search" page to query the full-text database and find reports.

Match any term
wilde verdict matches "wilde" or "verdict."
Wildcards
crim* matches "crime" and "criminal".
s?t matches "sit" and "sat" but not "saat."
match all terms
+wilde +verdict matches paragraphs which contain both wilde and verdict.
Exclude some terms
-wilde +verdict matches paragraphs which do not contain wilde but do contain verdict.
Phrase searches
"at the witness box"
Proximity search
"wilde queensberry"~10 matches paragraphs which contain the words wilde and queensberry no more than 10 words apart.
Fuzzy search
persecution~ matches words which are similar to persecution.
persecution~0.9 matches words which are closer to persecution than persecution~0.1 would.
Booleans
queensberry AND clarke finds paragraphs that contain both queensberry and clarke
Grouping booleans
(queensberry OR wilde) AND clarke

Comparing the Reports

The "Compare" page allows comparison of passages from any two news reports from the collection.

To compare two different pieces of text, enter them into the two fields below by copy and paste, and then select the Measure button.The text-comparison tool will produce text similarity scores based on levenshtein and cosine measures and highlight the differences between the two texts.

News Networks

These network graphs draw on matching reports data generated within the archive to visualise news flows about the Wilde trials.

Select the “Newspapers Networks Gephi” link to see the reports network visualization.

Each newspaper forms a node in the network, indicated by a dot on the graph. Newspapers are connected - or networked - to each other based on whether documents or paragraphs from them match. For example, if the New York Heraldhas any matches with the Philadelphia Times, they are networked or connected. Selecting a newspaper node will bring up metadata about its place in the network.
